Inside a Seized Scam Compound in Cambodia, the Machinery of Exploitation Comes Into View

A rare look behind the walls of the trafficking-fuelled fraud industry sweeping Southeast Asia, and the people fighting to dismantle it.

By Not For Sale Field Report · 9 min read · Today

Behind a high concrete wall on the edge of a Cambodian border town, a compound that once advertised itself as a casino resort tells a very different story up close. Inside, investigators describe rooms of workers held against their will, made to run online fraud operations targeting victims thousands of kilometres away.

The scale is what stops people first. What looks like an ordinary office block is, on closer inspection, a closed system designed to keep people inside, working, and invisible. The same forces that traffic a person across a border can keep them chained to a desk and a script.

“The walls are not just to keep people out. They are built to keep people in.”

How the pipeline works

The recruitment rarely looks like coercion at the start. A job listing promises good pay for customer-service work abroad. Travel is arranged, documents are taken on arrival, and the promised role turns out to be something else entirely. By the time a person understands what they have walked into, the exits are gone.

This is the upstream vulnerability Not For Sale works to close before it is ever exploited: the lack of safe, stable opportunity that makes a too-good-to-be-true offer worth the risk. Prevention is cheaper, and more humane, than rescue.

What changes the equation

Crackdowns matter, but enforcement alone moves the problem rather than ending it. Lasting change comes from the slower work: building local economies that offer dignity, supporting survivors through reintegration, and holding the supply chains that profit from forced labour to account.

That is the through-line of every project in this network, from prevention programs to survivor-led enterprise. The compound behind the wall is a symptom. The work is upstream.
